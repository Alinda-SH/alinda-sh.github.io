My Motivation

Performance motivation

I am reasonably intrinsically motivated and I am are reasonably motivated extrinsically. The fact that I am reasonably intrinsically motivated means that I really enjoy studying. But I don't really like it either. The fact that I am motivated to a reasonable degree means that I also take into account what I can achieve with my studies: a diploma, money, status or the recognition of the people I love. In itself there is nothing wrong with my motivation.

Career Anchor

Most points I gave to the role: Creativity (21 points)
For someone who has creativity as a career anchor, producing new ideas or products is the primary goal. The motive is to be innovative. A working environment that is procedural and leaves little room for personal initiative can be experienced as very restrictive. Creative people often prefer to work independently or in small teams.

Then I indicated the most points: Independence (15 points)
Autonomy is an important driver. People with this career anchor are all about independence and being able to make their own decisions. It is therefore not surprising that these people are often their own boss or would like to become one. They are therefore guided in their career choice by the question to what extent they can go their own way or work independently.

The least number of points I gave for: Wealth (6 points)
Wealth as a career anchor says something about the importance one places on material reward. Earning money is often the most important reason to work. A high income and earning more is the motivation for these people to make career choices. A high or higher income is an important measure of success for this person.

Career anchor

Conclusion

I am reasonably intrinsically motivated and I am are reasonably motivated extrinsically. My most important career anchors are creativity and independence, and my least important anchor is wealth.

According to author and speaker Kendra Cherry (Cherry, What is motivation?, 2020) is motivation a process that initiates, guides and maintains goal-oriented behaviours. It is what causes you to act, whether it is getting a glass of water to reduce thirst or reading a book to gain knowledge.
Motivation involves the emotional, biological, cognitive, and social forces that initiate and activate behaviour. In everyday practise, the term motivation is often used to describe why a person does something.
Cherry claims motivation doesnot only refer to the factors that activate behaviour, it also involves the aspects that direct and maintain these goal-directed actions, though such causes are rarely directly observable). As an outcome, we frequently have to infer the reasons why people do things they do based on observable behaviours.
